## **IV. Inflation Dynamics: The Exogenous Supply Shock Transmission**

The current ![][image2] Composite Inflation Z-score is not an artifact of broad-based macroeconomic overheating or excessive consumer credit creation; rather, it is the direct, mechanical manifestation of a geopolitical crisis intersecting with fragile global supply chains. In March 2026, the annual headline inflation rate jumped to 3.3%, the highest level since May 2024, representing a sharp and undeniable acceleration from the 2.4% prints recorded in both January and February.2

The internal distribution of the Consumer Price Index data reveals the exact transmission mechanism of this inflationary impulse. Energy costs surged by 12.5% on an annual basis, fueled by an 18.9% increase in gasoline prices and a staggering 44.2% jump in fuel oil.2 On a month-over-month basis, consumer prices rose 0.9%—the largest monthly gain since June 2022—boosted by a 21.2% explosion in gas prices at the pump, which hit a national average of $4.23 to $4.30 per gallon depending on the geographic region.4

Core inflation, which strips out volatile food and energy components, remains sticky but comparatively subdued at 2.6% year-over-year.2 However, the divergence between headline and core inflation in a severe supply-shock environment is highly transitory. Energy is a foundational input cost for the entire economy, and its price elasticity affects nearly every node in the supply chain. As noted by Chevron CEO Mike Wirth, jet fuel prices have surged 50% to $3.61 a gallon, instantly translating into a 15% increase in domestic airfares.22 Furthermore, the Producer Price Index (PPI) Final Demand year-over-year reading of 3.95% indicates massive pipeline inflation building in the wholesale sector. Manufacturers are currently absorbing these costs, but they will inevitably pass them onto consumers to preserve operating margins, causing Core CPI to systematically drift upward over the next two quarters.

The root cause of this inflationary impulse is the ongoing U.S.-Iran war and the subsequent blockade of the Strait of Hormuz.12 The Strait is the vital transit chokepoint for approximately 20% of the world's seaborne crude oil and liquefied natural gas (LNG) exports.4 The U.S. naval blockade, initiated by the Trump administration to throttle Iran's economy, has triggered what the International Energy Agency characterizes as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market".4

The immediate market reaction has driven Brent crude to $114.60 a barrel, up nearly 25% from mid-April lows.4 The physical supply destruction is immense; Vitol CEO Russell Hardy estimates that between 600 million to 1 billion barrels of oil production will be permanently lost due to the conflict.12 Furthermore, Iran has threatened that if the standoff persists, the world must prepare for oil prices to reach $200 per barrel, echoing the apocalyptic energy crises of the 1970s.13

This dynamic wholly validates the heavy 30% weight assigned to the 5-Year Breakeven Inflation rate in the Merrill Lynch composite algorithm. Currently sitting at 2.69%, the 5-Year Breakeven indicates that bond markets are pricing in structural, long-term inflation far above the Fed's 2.0% target. The market inherently understands that geopolitical blockades are not resolved cleanly, and the forced restructuring of global energy supply chains will maintain a high floor under commodity prices, locking the economy into the eastern hemisphere of the Investment Clock (rising inflation) for the foreseeable future.

## **VIII. Historical Analogs: Navigating Geopolitical Supply Shocks**

To understand the trajectory of asset prices following an energy-induced Overheat phase, it is imperative to examine highly correlated historical analogs. The current macroeconomic environment—defined by a geopolitical energy blockade, soaring headline inflation, late-cycle growth resilience, and an uncertain Fed transition—most closely mirrors the oil shocks of the 1970s and the 2008 commodity super-spike.

The primary analog is the 1970s energy crisis.1 In 1973, following the Yom Kippur War, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC) proclaimed an oil embargo, severely constraining global supply and quadrupling oil prices. Similarly, in 1979, the Iranian Revolution triggered a second massive supply shock. During both periods, U.S. economic growth was initially strong but rapidly decelerated as energy costs paralyzed industrial output and consumer spending. Inflation Z-scores skyrocketed, cementing a structural Stagflation phase that lasted for years.

During these 1970s periods, broad equity indices suffered horrific drawdowns in real terms. The S\&P 500 lost nearly 50% of its value between 1973 and 1974\. Long-duration government bonds experienced massive capital destruction as yields surged to account for double-digit inflation. Conversely, commodities (particularly gold and oil) and energy-related equities delivered parabolic, multi-hundred-percent returns, acting as the sole safe harbor for capital preservation.1

A secondary, more modern analog is the first half of 2008\. While the underlying catalyst was different (a synchronized global demand boom rather than a geopolitical blockade), the resulting price action is highly comparable.13 By mid-2008, the U.S. housing market was already fracturing, causing growth to decelerate, but Brent crude spiked to an all-time record high of $147 per barrel.13 The Investment Clock was pinned tightly on the Stagflation/Overheat border. Central banks, terrified by headline inflation, initially refused to cut rates aggressively. When the oil bubble burst and the true extent of the financial rot was revealed, the global economy violently shifted from Stagflation into deep Reflation/Recession. Equities and commodities crashed in unison over the ensuing six months.

If the Strait of Hormuz blockade is resolved rapidly in 2026, oil prices will collapse, mimicking the back-half of 2008, which would trigger a massive deflationary bust and force the clock immediately into the Reflation quadrant. However, if the conflict drags into a multi-year war of attrition, the 1970s analog will take precedence, necessitating a prolonged, structural overweight in hard assets and commodity equities to survive a lost decade of real equity returns.
