### **Phase Validation: The Empirical Arrival of Stagflation**

The Merrill Lynch Investment Clock is a foundational macroeconomic framework that categorizes the business cycle into four distinct phases—Reflation, Recovery, Overheat, and Stagflation—based entirely on the directional momentum of economic growth and inflation relative to their long-term trends.2 By standardizing disparate economic indicators into composite Z-scores, the model strips away absolute nominal values to focus purely on the standard deviations from the mean, providing a mathematically rigorous assessment of the business cycle.28 As of April 30, 2026, the algorithmic signals and underlying economic data decisively confirm that the United States economy has entered Phase 4: Stagflation.

Mathematically, Stagflation is defined by a negative (below-trend) growth environment coupled with a positive (above-trend) inflation environment.2 The current quantitative coordinates for the US economy place the Composite Growth Z-score at \-0.049 and the Composite Inflation Z-score at \+0.131. Positioned at 110.5 degrees on the Investment Clock face, the economy has officially crossed the vertical axis from the "Overheat" quadrant into contractionary growth territory while remaining elevated on the inflation axis.

The assertion that US economic growth has fallen below trend is heavily supported by both retrospective revisions and real-time nowcasting models. The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) issued a severe downward revision to fourth-quarter 2025 real GDP, slashing the initial estimate of 1.4% to a mere 0.5% annualized rate.1 This deceleration has bled directly into the first half of 2026\. The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model, a highly regarded real-time tracker of economic output, estimates Q1 2026 real GDP growth at an anemic 1.3% as of early April, representing a stark departure from the robust expansion witnessed in the previous year.3

### **Cycle Direction and Momentum: A Geopolitically Induced Counter-Clockwise Shock**

Exogenous shocks can force the clock hand to move erratically, skip phases, or rotate counter-clockwise against the natural economic grain.20

The six-month trajectory of the US economy from late 2025 into early 2026 provides a textbook example of a shock-induced cycle disruption.

Supply shocks are uniquely devastating in macroeconomics because they shift the aggregate supply curve inward, simultaneously destroying economic output (crushing growth) and driving up input costs (spiking inflation). ### **Inflation Dynamics: Supply Shocks, The Hormuz Blockade, and Supercore Resilience**

The trajectory of US inflation in the spring of 2026 is defined by a sharp divergence between extreme headline volatility and stubborn, sticky core components, exacerbated by unprecedented fiscal policy interventions.

#### **The Exogenous Oil Shock and Headline Inflation**

The primary driver pushing the Inflation Z-score into positive territory (+0.131) has been the global energy sector. Following the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) and Brent crude oil prices surged violently, with Brent briefly touching $119 a barrel in March.5 This translated directly to the consumer, resulting in the largest monthly gasoline price increase (21.2%) since 1967, and pushing headline CPI to 3.3%.1

Furthermore, pipeline inflation through the Producer Price Index (PPI) surged as global shipping and fertilizer supplies were stranded, threatening to pass through to retail food and core goods prices over the coming months.42 The March 2026 PPI climbed 4.0% year-over-year, validating fears that the energy shock was bleeding into broader manufacturing input costs.4

#### **Core and Supercore Resilience**

While headline inflation surged, Core CPI (excluding food and energy) remained relatively contained at 2.6% year-over-year.1 However, "supercore" inflation—services excluding housing and energy—remains stubbornly elevated around a 4.5% annualized rate.43 This stickiness is a byproduct of a tight labor market where wage growth continues to exert upward pressure on service sector pricing. In a classic stagflationary "wage-price spiral," companies facing compressed margins due to high input costs attempt to pass those costs onto consumers, embedding inflation deeper into the domestic economy.25 Furthermore, market-implied inflation expectations, as measured by the 5-Year Breakeven Inflation rate, remain elevated at 2.59%, indicating that bond markets anticipate price pressures remaining structurally above the Fed's 2.0% target for the foreseeable future.

### **Growth Dynamics: Moderation Beneath the Surface and Fiscal Stimulus**

While the inflation metrics flash warning signs, the broader macroeconomic foundation of the United States is undeniably deteriorating, dragging the Composite Growth Z-score below zero. However, the severity of this contraction is being masked by massive, unprecedented fiscal interventions.

#### **Output Contraction and the Yield Curve**

As previously established, the collapse of Q4 2025 GDP to 0.5% and the weak Q1 2026 Atlanta Fed GDPNow forecast of 1.3% underscore a rapid deceleration in real economic output.1 The industrial and manufacturing sectors are bearing the brunt of this slowdown, with new export orders and backlog orders both contracting in the latest ISM reports.12

Despite the profound deceleration in GDP growth, traditional recessionary alarm bells within the financial plumbing remain surprisingly muted. The 10-year to 2-year Treasury yield curve (10Y-2Y), widely regarded as the most reliable leading indicator of a recession when inverted, currently sits at a positive spread of \+0.54%.26 The curve had been deeply inverted from mid-2022 to mid-2024, but has since bear-steepened. A steepening curve following an inversion is historically the exact moment when recessions begin; however, the massive sovereign debt issuance required to fund government deficits has artificially driven up long-end yields (term premium), partially distorting the curve's traditional signal.14

Furthermore, corporate credit markets show no signs of systemic distress. The ICE BofA US High Yield Option-Adjusted Spread (OAS) remains incredibly tight at roughly 2.85%.27 In a typical deep recession, high-yield spreads blow out above 600-800 basis points as default risks soar. The tightness of these spreads in April 2026 suggests that corporate balance sheets remain well-capitalized and that the current economic phase represents a grinding, margin-compressing stagnation rather than a catastrophic deflationary collapse.

#### **The Fiscal Wildcard: The OBBBA**

Complicating the growth and inflation dynamic is the implementation of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), a $5 trillion fiscal package signed into law in July 2025\. The legislation permanently extends the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), raises state and local tax (SALT) deduction caps, and introduces new deductions for overtime pay and tips.6

Because many of these provisions were applied retroactively, the IRS implementation in early 2026 is generating a massive, front-loaded surge in tax refunds for US consumers, estimated to boost disposable income by upwards of $150 billion.47 This injection of fiscal stimulus acts as an economic "sugar rush," artificially inflating aggregate consumer demand exactly when the Federal Reserve is attempting to cool the economy. The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) and independent models from the Yale Budget Lab estimate that the OBBBA will continuously add upward pressure on prices, forcing long-term interest rates structurally higher to accommodate the soaring federal deficits, which are projected to reach over 6% of GDP.14 While the OBBBA adds roughly 0.2% to GDP growth in the short term (2025-2027), the long-term crowding out of private investment is expected to stunt potential output moving forward.14

### **Historical Analogs: Echoes of the 1970s Supply Shocks and 1995 Financial Resilience**

To properly contextualize the April 2026 macroeconomic coordinates, it is highly instructive to compare the current Z-score mix, Federal Reserve stance, and exogenous drivers to previous eras. The current environment is a hybrid, blending the supply-shock origins of the 1970s with the resilient financial plumbing of the mid-1990s.

#### **The 1973 and 1979 Oil Shocks (The Inflation Analog)**

The most direct comparison for the *cause* of the 2026 stagflationary environment is the 1970s.23 In 1973 (the OPEC oil embargo) and 1979 (the Iranian Revolution), geopolitical disruptions in the Middle East choked off the global energy supply. Much like the 2026 Hormuz blockade, these events caused energy prices to skyrocket overnight.

* **Macro Mix:** In both eras, the sudden spike in input costs fractured corporate productivity and compressed margins. Growth plummeted while headline inflation soared into double digits.  
* **Asset Performance:** During the 1970s stagflation, traditional 60/40 portfolios (equities and bonds) were decimated. Real returns for equities were negative for a decade, and fixed-coupon bonds were destroyed by inflation. Cash and commodities (specifically gold and energy) were the only true safe havens.23  
* **Divergence:** The critical difference today is central bank credibility. In the 1970s, the Federal Reserve allowed inflation to run rampant before acting decisively. In 2026, Jerome Powell's Fed has maintained a restrictive 3.50%-3.75% rate specifically to prevent a 1970s-style unanchoring.8

#### **The 1995 Mid-Cycle Adjustment (The Growth & Policy Analog)**

While the *origin* of the 2026 inflation mirrors the 1970s, the underlying resilience of the US corporate sector and the proactive nature of the Federal Reserve closely resemble 1994-1995.22

* **Macro Mix:** In 1994, the Fed hiked rates aggressively to preempt inflation. By early 1995, growth had decelerated significantly (retail sales flattened, industrial production stalled), leading economists to predict a severe recession.22  
* **Asset Performance:** The Fed's successful orchestration of a "soft landing" in 1995, pivoting to rate cuts just as inflation cooled, ultimately paved the way for the massive equity bull market of the late 1990s.  
* **Convergence:** Today, the positive slope of the 10Y-2Y yield curve (+0.54%) and the historically tight high-yield credit spreads (2.85%) heavily suggest that the US economy is experiencing a painful but manageable mid-cycle slowdown, rather than a systemic, balance-sheet-destroying recession (such as 2008).26

**Analog Synthesis:** The 2026 economy is enduring a 1970s-style geopolitical shock, but defending against it with 1990s-style financial resilience and central bank vigilance. If the geopolitical shock resolves, the financial markets are primed to follow the 1995 trajectory rather than the 1970s lost decade.

#### **Leading Indicators to Monitor**

To accurately anticipate the timing and velocity of the transition from Stagflation to Reflation, market participants must relentlessly monitor the following high-frequency data points:

1. **Commodity Price Action (Brent/WTI Futures):** The single most critical variable. Sustained crude prices below $85 a barrel will guarantee a drop in the Inflation Z-score, forcing a move toward Reflation. Any spike back above $95 indicates stagflationary entrenchment.  
2. **ISM Manufacturing \- New Orders vs. Inventories Spread:** A widening spread where Inventories exceed New Orders will indicate accelerating economic contraction, confirming the Growth Z-score will remain deeply negative and preventing a move into the Recovery quadrant.  
3. **Initial Jobless Claims (4-Week Moving Average):** A sustained break above the 230,000 level would signal that margin compression has finally forced widespread corporate layoffs, breaking the back of the wage-price spiral and giving the Federal Reserve the undeniable green light to initiate rate cuts, thus officially ushering in the Reflation phase.
