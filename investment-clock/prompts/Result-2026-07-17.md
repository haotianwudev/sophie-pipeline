# **Merrill Lynch Investment Clock Weekly Analysis: Exogenous Shocks and the Counter-Clockwise Rotation**

## **1\. Executive Summary**

As of mid-July 2026, the United States economy is experiencing a highly anomalous, exogenous shock-driven transition within the Merrill Lynch Investment Clock framework, shifting counter-clockwise from an "Overheat" regime into a fragile "Recovery" phase. This transition does not stem from structural macroeconomic cooling or an organic business cycle progression, but rather from the artificial collapse of global energy prices following the June 17, 2026, Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU), which temporarily reopened the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. Concurrently, the Federal Reserve under newly appointed Chairman Kevin Warsh has initiated a hawkish regime change, abandoning forward guidance, holding the effective federal funds rate at 3.63%, and embracing a bear-steepening yield curve. While algorithmic signals advocate for aggressive equity allocations characteristic of a standard economic recovery, the underlying geopolitical fragility and restrictive monetary policy demand a highly defensive, barbell approach to asset allocation, pairing secular technology growth with tactical commodity hedges.

### **Growth Dynamics: The GDPNow vs. Credit Market Divergence**

The Composite Growth Z-score of \+0.118 sits precariously close to the zero-line boundary separating economic expansion from contraction. Underlying this metric is a profound divergence between high-frequency labor data and real-time gross domestic product estimates.  
The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model for the second quarter of 2026 collapsed dramatically, falling from a robust 4.3% estimate in mid-June to a sluggish 1.3% by July 810. This deceleration was primarily driven by negative revisions to inventory investments and deteriorating net exports following US Census Bureau wholesale trade reports10. Typically, a growth nowcast shedding more than half its value in three weeks signifies the onset of a recession11.  
However, credit markets and labor metrics vehemently contradict this narrative. Initial jobless claims fell to 212,000 for the week ending July 4, representing a 6.5% decline over the previous 30 days, while the headline unemployment rate held steady at 4.2%11. Firms facing an imminent demand collapse do not hoard labor; payroll is the fastest variable cost to cut, making weekly claims a highly sensitive front-edge indicator of economic distress11. Furthermore, high-yield credit spreads tightened by 8 basis points to 2.7, and the Chicago Fed’s financial conditions index loosened to \-0.51511. Lenders, who own the downside risk of corporate defaults, are aggressively pricing out a growth scare. Industrial output confirms this resilience, with the ISM Manufacturing PMI registering 53.3 in June—its sixth consecutive month of expansion, driven by strong new orders and a massive 25% year-over-year surge in high-tech business spending directed toward artificial intelligence infrastructure12. Therefore, the positive Growth Z-score is validated: the economy is expanding, propelled by a narrow but potent AI capital expenditure boom that masks cyclical inventory liquidations.

### **Inflation Dynamics: The Disinflationary Mirage**

The Composite Inflation Z-score of \-0.606 is fundamentally distorted. While the headline CPI fell by 0.4% in June (the largest such decrease since 2020), this was entirely a function of plunging energy prices resulting from the Islamabad MoU8. The underlying structural inflation metrics remain highly restrictive and sticky. Core CPI, which strips out volatile food and energy components, is running at 2.25% to 2.6% year-over-year, consistently above the Federal Reserve's 2% target15. Producer Price Index (PPI) final demand sits at a hot 4.77% year-over-year, indicating robust pipeline price pressures16.  
Furthermore, the 5-year breakeven inflation rate—a measure of market-implied forward inflation expectations—remains anchored at 2.27%, demonstrating that the bond market does not believe the recent headline drop is indicative of a permanent return to pre-2020 price stability11. The divergence between the algorithmic reading (deep disinflation) and the economic reality (sticky core inflation) is the primary reason the current Recovery phase designation carries only a 40.0% confidence level.

### **Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy: The Warsh Regime**

Asset allocation within the Investment Clock relies heavily on the predictable reaction function of central banks. In a standard Recovery phase, central banks lower interest rates and provide ample liquidity to stimulate aggregate demand1. The current macroeconomic environment is operating under the exact opposite conditions due to a profound institutional regime change at the Federal Reserve.  
Following his confirmation in May 2026, Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh has forcefully dismantled the flexible average inflation targeting framework established by his predecessor, Jerome Powell17. In his July congressional testimonies, Warsh stated unequivocally that "inflation is a choice" and declared that the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) has "no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation"12. Warsh refused to interpret the June headline CPI drop as a victory, insisting that the Fed will not "pass the buck" to volatile supply shocks and remains rigidly committed to achieving the 2% structural target12.  
Reflecting this hawkish orthodoxy, the FOMC unanimously held the target federal funds rate at 3.50% to 3.75% in June20. The effective federal funds rate (EFFR) remains actively traded at 3.63%22. More importantly, the June Summary of Economic Projections (the "dot plot") revealed that the median expectation for the end of 2026 increased from 3.4% to 3.8%24. This implies that the Fed's next move is more likely to be a rate *hike* rather than a cut, fundamentally contradicting the accommodative monetary posture required for a sustained Investment Clock Recovery.  
To institutionalize this policy shift, Warsh has assembled five independent task forces composed of elite external advisors (including Mervyn King, Raghuram Rajan, Marc Andreessen, and Karen Dynan)26.

### **The Bear Steepener and Yield Curve Dynamics**

A critical recessionary indicator—the inversion of the US Treasury yield curve—has recently reversed. Throughout much of 2023 and 2024, the curve was deeply inverted, traditionally signaling an impending economic contraction36. As of mid-July 2026, the curve has decisively un-inverted. The 10-year Treasury yield sits at 4.57%, the 2-year yield is at 4.16%, and the 3-month bill is at 3.79%38. This results in a positive 10y-2y spread of \+41 basis points and a 10y-3m spread of \+78 basis points38.  
However, the specific mechanics of this un-inversion present a distinct macroeconomic risk. Yield curves typically un-invert via a *bull steepener*, wherein short-term rates plummet as the central bank aggressively cuts the federal funds rate to rescue a deteriorating economy35. The 2026 phenomenon is a **bear steepener**: the curve has steepened because long-term rates have risen faster than short-term rates35.  
This bear steepening is driven by resilient domestic data preventing near-term rate cuts, combined with an expanding term premium. The US Treasury is issuing a deluge of long-dated debt to fund persistent fiscal deficits, forcing investors to demand higher yields to absorb the duration risk34. Simultaneously, expectations that Warsh's balance sheet task force will accelerate quantitative tightening removes the central bank as a structural buyer of long-end duration27. While a positively sloped yield curve improves bank lending incentives (borrowing short at 3.79% to lend long at 5.09%), a bear steepener driven by rising real rates rather than inflation breakevens poses a severe threat to equity valuations35. The equity risk premium has compressed to roughly 4%, a 15-year low, indicating that the stock market possesses almost zero margin of safety if the 10-year yield breaches the 5.00% threshold35.

The most immediate existential risk to the current Recovery phase is the fragility of the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding. Mediated by Pakistan and Qatar, the 14-point framework signed on June 17, 2026, established a 60-day ceasefire, reopening the Strait of Hormuz and lifting the US naval blockade on Iranian ports6. This agreement is solely responsible for the collapse in oil prices and the resulting negative Inflation Z-score7.  
However, the agreement is buckling under structural instability. While direct kinetic actions between the US and Iran have paused, Israel continues strikes against Hezbollah in Lebanon, threatening to ignite a wider regional conflagration43. Furthermore, compliance disputes are escalating rapidly. Iran has threatened to resume blocking commercial shipping, citing Israeli ceasefire violations and accusing the US of failing to implement phased sanctions relief7. In retaliation, the US revoked a critical Iranian oil sales waiver on July 7, and President Trump announced intentions to reimpose the blockade, proposing a 20% protection fee on cargo passing through the Strait7.  
With the 60-day window set to expire in mid-August 2026, the probability of the MoU collapsing is exceptionally high6. If the Strait of Hormuz—through which 20% of global oil and liquefied natural gas flows—closes again, energy prices will spike violently7. This would instantly erase the \-0.606 Inflation Z-score, dragging the Investment Clock directly back into the Overheat or Stagflation quadrants.

## **7\. Historical Analog Comparison**

To properly contextualize the anomalous 2026 macroeconomic environment, we must evaluate historical periods that match the current parameters: elevated but falling headline inflation, a hawkish Federal Reserve targeting structural price stability, a bear-steepening yield curve, and extreme exogenous energy volatility.

### **Analog 1: The 1994-1995 Soft Landing**

The most accurate domestic monetary and technological analog is the 1994-1995 economic cycle. In 1994, Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan executed a preemptive, aggressive rate-hiking cycle to crush nascent inflation expectations, effectively doubling the federal funds rate29. This resulted in a severe bear market in bonds and a bear-steepening yield curve. However, by 1995, the yield curve normalized, economic growth slowed but narrowly avoided a recession, and the US economy entered a massive, multi-year expansion driven by a paradigm-shifting general-purpose technology—the commercialization of the Internet29.  
Today, Kevin Warsh is attempting the exact same maneuver. By abandoning forward guidance and holding rates restrictive to enforce orthodox price stability, Warsh is risking short-term volatility to anchor long-term expectations12. Furthermore, he is explicitly relying on the productivity gains of a new general-purpose technology (Artificial Intelligence) to facilitate a soft landing, mirroring the mid-1990s dynamic12. Following the 1994 turbulence, asset prices in the tech sector experienced unprecedented growth over the subsequent 36 months, suggesting massive upside for IYW if the Warsh Fed achieves its soft landing.

### **Analog 2: The 1990 Gulf War Exogenous Shock**

The geopolitical analog most closely resembling the 2026 data distortions is the 1990 Gulf War. Following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, global oil prices spiked violently, driving headline inflation higher and pushing the US economy into a brief recession. However, as the conflict rapidly resolved via Operation Desert Storm, oil prices collapsed, leading to a sudden, artificial disinflationary print53.  
The 2026 US-Iran conflict and the subsequent Islamabad MoU perfectly mirror this volatility. Just as in the early 1990s, headline macroeconomic indicators were entirely hijacked by Middle Eastern military dynamics, resulting in false dawn disinflationary prints that whip-sawed asset prices35. In 1990, investors who blindly followed algorithmic sector rotation models without accounting for the artificiality of the energy crash suffered severe capital misallocation. The 2026 environment demands the same skepticism; separating the noise of military-driven commodity fluctuations from the underlying structural health of the consumer and credit markets is paramount to surviving the current cycle.
